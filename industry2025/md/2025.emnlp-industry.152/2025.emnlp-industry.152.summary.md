# Summary

## TL;DR  
BullyBench is a youth‑in‑the‑loop cyber‑bullying dataset that combines co‑designed dialogues, LLM‑generated conversations, and expert‑validated intrinsic evaluation. It offers a gold‑standard test set (DGold) with fine‑grained labels for aggression, harm, and intent, and demonstrates baseline role‑classification performance using GPT‑2, RoBERTa, and Llama‑3.3. The work is industry‑track relevant because it delivers a realistic, ethically‑collected resource that can be directly applied to downstream cyber‑bullying detection systems.

## Problem & Motivation  
Cyberbullying (CB) is a relational, repetitive phenomenon that existing datasets treat as a binary classification problem, ignoring nuanced roles and dynamics. Current youth‑focused corpora rely on scripted role‑play, lack conversational realism, and provide little or no social plausibility evaluation. The AMiCA corpus, for example, is drawn from the outdated ASK.fm platform and suffers from severe class imbalance (e.g., Enabler F1 = 0.00). There is a pressing need for authentic, youth‑generated data that captures the full spectrum of CB roles (bully, victim, enabler, defender) and can be used in real‑world applications.

## Approach  
1. **Co‑design & Co‑research** – 12 Irish teens (6 M, 6 F, ages 15–16) participated in a five‑day “young research assistantship” to craft 15 CB vignettes and expand them into multi‑party dialogues.  
2. **LLM‑driven Generation (D1)** – Llama‑3.3‑70B‑Instruct was prompted with a multi‑layered template (character archetypes, controlled escalation, chain‑of‑thought) to produce 50‑message conversations for each vignette, paired with 10 youth‑generated problem phrases.  
3. **Human‑in‑the‑loop Translation (D2)** – Four professional translators (3 M, 1 F) from DataForce post‑edited French‑to‑English translations of the Ollagnier et al. (2022) corpus.  
4. **Three‑stage Annotation (DGold)** – Annotators labeled each message for (i) aggression type (covertly, overtly, no), (ii) harm type (threats, blackmail, personal attack, identity‑based harassment, body‑shaming, exclusion, sexual‑harassment, general insult, encouragement to bully, no harm), and (iii) intent type (sarcasm, attack, blame, defend, abet, instigate, gaslighting, no‑intent). Krippendorff’s α was reported for inter‑annotator agreement.  
5. **Intrinsic Evaluation** – Experts (content moderators, social scientists, youth psychologists) scored realism, relevance, coherence, and six dynamics sub‑dimensions (conflict, support, influence, humour, power‑dynamics, repetition) on a 5‑point Likert scale. Fleiss κ and PABAK‑OS were computed.  
6. **Extrinsic Benchmarking** – Role‑classification was formalised as predicting \(y_i \in \{bully, victim, enabler, defender\}\). Three paradigms were evaluated: (a) Direct multiclass baseline, (b) Hierarchical pairwise (bully/victim side then sub‑role), and (c) Prompt‑based LLM classification (zero‑, one‑, few‑shot). Baselines used fine‑tuned GPT‑2, RoBERTa, and Llama‑3.3.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Datasets**:  
  - **DGold** – 15 vignettes × 50 messages (≈750 messages) annotated for aggression, harm, intent.  
  - **D1** – Llama‑generated conversations (15 × 10 × 50 messages).  
  - **D2** – French‑to‑English translations of Ollagnier et al. (2022).  
  - **D3** – Verma et al. (2023) English youth CB dataset.  
- **Baselines**: GPT‑2, RoBERTa, Llama‑3.3.  
- **Metrics**: Krippendorff’s α, Fleiss κ, PABAK‑OS for intrinsic evaluation; accuracy/F1 (not explicitly reported) for role‑classification.  
- **Deployment Context**: Offline evaluation on the combined dataset \(D = D1 \cup D2 \cup D3\). No online deployment reported.
