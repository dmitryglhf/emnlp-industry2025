# Summary

## TL;DR  
The paper demonstrates that a supervised binary relevance classifier, trained on a small set of expert judgments from Daily Drilling Reports (DDRs), outperforms zero‑shot Large Language Models (LLMs) for automated relevance annotation in the oil and gas industry. The best classifier (NN + CQ + BGE + Hada) achieves an F1‑score of **0.860**, surpassing the second human annotator (0.793) and providing a practical, scalable path for building high‑quality IR test collections in specialized domains.

## Problem & Motivation  
- **Scarcity of annotated data**: Specialized domains like oil and gas lack high‑quality relevance judgments, hindering IR system development.  
- **Limitations of pooling**: Traditional TREC‑style pooling relies on many annotators and often yields sparse relevant pools when retrieval systems perform poorly.  
- **Human cost**: Manual annotation is expensive and slow; experts are scarce.  
- **LLM skepticism**: Prior work shows LLMs may align with their own biases rather than human judgment, raising concerns about using them as gold standards.

## Approach  
- **Dataset**: Daily Drilling Reports (DDRs) with 4‑point relevance scale, binarized into Relevant (1‑3) vs. Not Relevant (0).  
- **Supervised classifiers**:  
  - **Embedding models**: BGE‑multilingual‑gemma2 (BGE) and domain‑adapted PetroBERT (P‑BERT).  
  - **Interaction methods**: DocOnly, Concat, Absolute Difference (Diff), Hadamard Product (Hada), Cosine Similarity (CosSim).  
  - **Classifiers**: Logistic Regression (LR), SVM‑RBF, Random Forest (RF), Gradient Boosting (GB), Neural Network (NN).  
  - **Training strategies**: Cross‑Query (CQ), Per‑Query (PQ), Unseen Queries (UQ).  
- **LLM annotators**: GPT‑4o and GPT‑4o‑mini, evaluated in zero‑shot settings with two prompts: Generic (Baseline) and Domain‑Knowledge (Specialist).  
- **Evaluation metrics**: Precision, Recall, F1‑score, Cohen’s Kappa (κ).  
- **Retrieval systems**: BM25, BGE, mContriever, ColBERT, text‑embedding‑3‑large.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Data**: 5,066 query‑document pairs annotated by two human experts (HA1, HA2).  
- **Baselines**: Human annotators (HA1 as gold, HA2 for cross‑validation).  
- **Metrics**: F1, Precision, Recall, κ.  
- **Cross‑validation**: 5‑fold stratified (CQ), per‑query (PQ), leave‑one‑out (UQ).  
- **Deployment**: Offline evaluation; no online A/B‑tests reported.

## Results  
| Method | Precision | Recall | F1‑Score | κ (vs. HA2) |
|--------|-----------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Human Annotator 2 | 0.867 | 0.730 | 0.793 | – |
| **Best Classifier (NN + CQ + BGE + Hada)** | **0.860 ± 0.017** | **0.860 ± 0.020** | **0.860 ± 0.018** | **0.669** |
| GPT‑4o (Specialist) | 0.522 | 0.940 | 0.671 | 0.401 |
| GPT‑4o‑mini (Specialist) | 0.407 | 0.983 | 0.575 | 0.136 |

- **Human‑human agreement**: κ = 0.695.  
- **Classifier‑HA1 agreement**: κ = 0.785.  
- **LLM agreement**: κ ranged from 0.136 to 0.401, far below human agreement.  
- **Precision‑recall trade‑off**: LLMs exhibit very high recall (up to 0.983) but low precision, yielding lower F1 than classifiers.

## Industry Impact  
Not reported.

## Limitations  
- The supervised classifier inherits **annotator bias**; its high performance depends on the quality and consistency of the training data.  
- LLMs, while useful for broad candidate discovery, lack sufficient precision for final relevance annotation.  
- The study does not evaluate deployment at scale or in real‑time systems; results are limited to offline evaluation.  
- No discussion of cost, latency, or integration with existing IR pipelines is provided.
