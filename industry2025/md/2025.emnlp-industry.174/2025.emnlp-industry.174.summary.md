# Summary

## TL;DR  
GRIEVER is a lightweight, low‑resource, multi‑step graph‑based retriever for multi‑hop QA that does not rely on LLM calls and can retrieve relevant passages in a few hundred milliseconds. It outperforms conventional retrievers on MuSiQue, 2Wiki and HotpotQA, achieving R@5 of 0.456, 0.676 and 0.813 respectively, while running at least an order of magnitude faster than dense‑only baselines. When combined with agentic frameworks such as GEAR, it yields relative recall gains of up to 4.9 %.

## Problem & Motivation  
Multi‑hop question answering requires reasoning over multiple passages, but existing Retrieval‑augmented Generation (RAG) systems struggle because they rely on expensive LLM calls or sparse‑only retrieval. GraphRAG approaches align passages with extracted triples, yet they still incur high deployment costs. The paper addresses the need for an efficient, low‑resource retriever that can perform multi‑step retrieval within 100–500 ms while maintaining high recall.

## Approach  
GRIEVER builds an offline index with three components: **passages** (passage id, title, content, triples), **partial_triples** (subject‑predicate or predicate‑object pairs with complement entities), and **same_as** (synonyms with passage ids). During online retrieval, it performs:

1. **Relative clause splitting** – splits the query on relative‑clause connectors to generate sub‑queries.  
2. **Hybrid passage retrieval** – uses a composed retrieval strategy that first filters dense candidates with a sparse retriever (BM25) and then fuses dense and sparse results via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF). The composed retrieval further reduces dense candidates.  
3. **Synonym expansion** – expands joinable entities using the same_as index, filtered by retrieved passage ids.  
4. **Triple shortlisting** – retrieves triples from the partial_triples index and scores them with a lightweight vector‑based matcher on token‑level embeddings. Scoring follows equations (1) and (2), averaging predicate and entity scores and normalising by log‑frequency. Paths are scored as the average of constituent triple scores, and passage scores are the maximum product of triple and path scores.  

A heuristic query‑rewriting step removes matched entity mentions from the query and replaces them with the remaining unmatched entity in the corresponding triples, enabling the next retrieval iteration to focus on the next hop.

## Experimental Setup  
Experiments were run on three public multi‑hop QA datasets: **MuSiQue** (Trivedi et al., 2022), **2Wiki**, and **HotpotQA**. Baselines include BM25, Dense, RRFHybrid, Composed, and GRIEVER. Retrieval metrics are R@5, R@10, R@15; runtime metrics are ms@5, ms@10, ms@15. End‑to‑end QA metrics are EM and F1 for the top‑5 retrieved passages. ## Results  
| Dataset | Retriever | R@5 | R@10 | R@15 | ms@5 | ms@10 | ms@15 |
|---------|-----------|------|-------|-------|-------|--------|--------|
| MuSiQue | GRIEVER | 0.456 | 0.539 | 0.573 | 425±122 | 441±135 | 509±127 |
| 2Wiki   | GRIEVER | 0.676 | 0.738 | 0.751 | 435±93 | 476±77 | 603±77 |
| HotpotQA| GRIEVER | 0.813 | 0.909 | 0.937 | 273±59 | 300±49 | 337±53 |

GRIEVER outperforms BM25, Dense, RRFHybrid and Composed on all datasets. Compared to the full‑triples setting, GRIEVER (partial_triples) achieves 0.456/0.539/0.573 on MuSiQue versus 0.457/0.536/0.532 with full_triples, and 0.676/0.738/0.751 on 2Wiki versus 0.677/0.722/0.695 with full_triples. Runtime is an order of magnitude faster than dense‑only baselines (e.g., Dense ms@5 294±12 vs GRIEVER 425±122 on MuSiQue). End‑to‑end QA metrics (Table 2) show GRIEVER EM/F1 of 21.6/32.6 on MuSiQue and 46.2/59.8 on HotpotQA, outperforming BM25 (18.4/27.6 and 43.8/57.2) and Dense (15.2/25.4 and 42.1/55.0). When combined with the agentic framework GEAR, GRIEVER yields relative recall gains of up to 4.9 %.
