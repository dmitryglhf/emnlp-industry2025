# Summary

## TL;DR  
The paper introduces a persona‑driven user simulator built on large language models (LLMs) to evaluate a proactive sales chatbot, SALESBOT 2.0. By generating 100 realistic customer personas, goals, and four assertiveness/precision scenarios, the authors produce 5 200 simulated dialogues and show that the simulator faithfully follows persona, goal, and scenario constraints. Human and LLM‑based evaluations confirm high compliance, and the simulator reveals that cross‑selling can be added with minimal impact on satisfaction, varying by customer type.

## Problem & Motivation  
Real‑world conversational agents need scalable evaluation before release. Human evaluation is costly and slow, while rule‑based simulators lack nuance. Existing LLM‑based simulators are generic and not tailored to specific customer populations. The authors target a sales chatbot (SALESBOT 2.0) that must adapt to diverse customer profiles and proactively cross‑sell, requiring an evaluation framework that captures realistic customer behavior and measures impact on satisfaction.

## Approach  
1. **Persona Generation** – 100 customer personas for Orange Poland were created with Anthropic Claude 3.5 Sonnet v2, incorporating market data, demographics, and technology usage.  
2. **Goal Creation** – Two primary goals per persona (first‑time purchase, upgrade, replacement, multi‑device) were generated, ensuring realistic constraints.  
3. **Scenario Design** – Four scenarios per persona‑goal pair (assertiveness LOW/HIGH × precision LOW/HIGH) were produced, defining budget, decision style, and response expectations.  
4. **Simulator Pipeline** – A single LLM‑based user simulator (Google Gemini 1.5‑flash) receives the persona, goal, and scenario in its prompt and converses with SALESBOT 2.0.  
5. **Evaluation** – Human annotators (3) rate 104 dialogues on persona, goal, and scenario compliance. Eight open‑source LLMs and seven commercial LLMs evaluate all 5 200 dialogues, using the same criteria.

## Experimental Setup  
| Item | Details |
|------|----------|
| **SALESBOT 2.0** | GPT‑4o‑mini (same model as production SalesBot 1.0) |
| **Configurations** | 13 total: baseline R&D SalesBot 1.0 + 12 experimental variants (cross‑sell with accessories, cross‑sell with pre‑defined products, upsell; strategy: always attempt vs. profile‑based; timing: after base offer vs. after acceptance) |
| **User Simulator** | Gemini 1.5‑flash |
| **Dataset** | 100 personas × 2 goals × 4 scenarios → 5200 dialogues |
| **Human Evaluation** | 104 dialogues (2 % of total), 3 annotators, 1‑5 scale |
| **LM Evaluation** | 8 open‑source models (Qwen 32B/14B/7B, Llama‑3.1‑8B, Selene‑1‑Mini‑Llama‑3.1‑8B, DeepSeek‑R1‑Distill variants) + 7 commercial models (GPT‑o1, GPT‑o1‑mini, GPT‑o3, GPT‑o3‑mini, GPT‑4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet v2, Claude 3.7 Sonnet) |
| **Cost** | Total \$70 (≈\$7 on user simulations) |
| **Turn Limit** | 70 turns or user returns “FINISHED” |
| **Metrics** | User satisfaction, helpfulness, naturalness, proactiveness (bot); persona, goal, scenario compliance (simulator) |

## Results  
### User Simulation Quality  
| Metric | Persona | Goal | Scenario |
|--------|---------|------|----------|
| Mean | 4.89 ± 0.31 | 4.76 ± 0.51 | 4.45 ± 0.73 |
| 5/5 ratings | 89.4 % | 79.2 % | 56.7 % |
| 4/5 ratings | 9.9 % | 17.6 % | 33.3 % |
| ≤3/5 ratings | 0.6 % | 3.2 % | 9.9 % |
| Exact agreement | 82.1 % | 77.2 % | 53.2 % |
| ±1 agreement | 98.7 % | 97.4 % | 91.7 % |
| ICC(2,k) | 0.14 | 0.66 | 0.57 |
| Krippendorff α | 0.05 | 0.42 | 0.31 |

LM‑based evaluation with **DeepSeek‑R1‑Distill‑Qwen‑14B** (best alignment with human ratings) on all 5 200 dialogues yielded:
- Over 99.7 % dialogues rated ≥ 4.  
- Goal compliance 93.73 %, persona 84.83 %, scenario 77.31 %.  

Commercial models: GPT‑4o and GPT‑o3‑mini had MAE = 0.11 on persona compliance; Claude 3.7 Sonnet had MAE = 0.24 on goal compliance. All commercial models achieved perfect ±1 agreement for persona compliance and > 89 % agreement on other dimensions.  

### SALESBOT 2.0 Performance  
Human and LLM judges assessed user satisfaction, helpfulness, naturalness, and proactiveness across the 13 configurations. The paper reports that cross‑selling strategies can be added with minimal satisfaction impact, varying by customer type, but does not provide explicit numeric improvements per configuration.
