# Summary

## TL;DR
ProCut compresses large prompt templates by treating them as collections of semantically coherent segments and pruning low‑utility ones via attribution estimation. On five public benchmarks and two production pipelines, ProCut cuts prompt length by up to 78 % while preserving or slightly improving task performance—up to 62 % better than alternative compression baselines—making it a practical, training‑free solution for industry‑scale LLM serving.

## Problem & Motivation
Large‑scale LLM deployments routinely use prompts that grow to thousands of tokens as teams add instructions, few‑shot examples, and heuristic rules. This bloat inflates inference latency and API costs, can dilute critical instructions (reducing accuracy), and creates maintenance debt due to overlapping or conflicting sections. Existing hard (token‑level removal, paraphrasing) and soft (embedding‑based) compression methods either break placeholders, produce disfluent text, or require retraining per LLM, limiting their industrial applicability. A flexible, model‑agnostic compression method that preserves interpretability and cross‑model transferability is needed.

## Approach
1. **Segmentation** – The prompt template is split into *M* disjoint segments (sentences/paragraphs). Three strategies are offered: pre‑defined, structure‑aware, and LLM‑driven segmentation (the latter prompts an LLM to partition unstructured templates such as those produced by TextGrad).  
2. **Attribution Estimation** – Each segment is treated as a binary feature. Perturbation‑based attribution methods are applied:  
   * SHAP (Monte‑Carlo marginal contributions)  
   * Leave‑One‑Out (performance drop when a segment is removed)  
   * LASSO regression (sparse linear fit to masked prompts)  
   * Greedy forward selection  
   Additionally, a novel **LLM‑driven attribution estimator** is introduced: the LLM is asked to generate a small set of candidate masks, each evaluated on the training set; the resulting feedback refines the ranking in a probe‑and‑test loop, reducing the number of LLM calls from *O(M)* to a constant.  
3. **Pruning** – Retain the top ⌊r M⌋ segments (r ∈ [0, 1] is a user‑defined compression ratio). The retained segments keep their original order to preserve context.

## Experimental Setup
| Dataset | Tasks | Train | Test | Metric |
|---------|-------|-------|------|--------|
| GSM8K | – | 20 | 100 | Exact Match |
| SQuAD | – | 20 | 100 | F1 |
| HumanEval | – | 20 | 100 | pass@1 |
| BBH | Color Reasoning, Penguins, Temporal Sequence, College Medicine, College Math | 20 | 100/75 | Exact Match |
| MMLU | Anatomy, Astronomy | 20 | 100 | Exact Match |

- **Model**: GPT‑4.1 mini via OpenAI API (April 2025 release).  
- **Settings**: temperature = 0, 4000‑token context window, 10 concurrent threads.  
- **Compression ratios**: r ∈ {25 %, 50 %, 75 %} for RQ1/2; r ∈ {40 %, 60 %, 80 %} for RQ3.  
- **Baselines**: Vanilla LLM, Selective Context, Random Selection, Brute‑Force Oracle, token‑level methods LLMLingua and LLMLingua‑2.  
- **Evaluation**: Average task metric over the test set; each setting run 5 times and averaged.

**LLM‑driven attribution** (Figure 4): achieves NDCG comparable to SHAP while reducing runtime by 52 % (LLM‑as‑Ranker 2 Shots) and 80 % (LLM‑as‑Ranker 4 Shots) relative to SHAP, LOO, and LASSO.

**Integration with TextGrad** (Figure 6): after 3 iterations, ProCut‑regularized prompts have token counts 27 %, 47 %, 66 % of the TextGrad‑only group (r = 40 %, 60 %, 80 %) while maintaining performance 0.815, 0.819, 0.813 versus baseline 0.813.

## Industry Impact
| Use Case | Token Reduction | Cost Savings | Performance Impact |
|----------|-----------------|--------------|--------------------|
| Intent Classification | 73 % | ~\$7 K | Preserved |
| Qualification Assessment | 84 % | ~\$8 K | Slightly improved |

ProCut was applied to two high‑traffic production pipelines: an intent‑classification prompt (reduced from ~2,200 tokens to ~600 tokens, 73 % reduction) and a qualification‑assessment prompt (reduced to ~300 tokens, 84 % reduction). Both maintained or improved accuracy while cutting inference cost.

## Limitations
- Attribution methods require many LLM calls; the proposed LLM‑driven estimator reduces this to a constant number but still depends on LLM inference.  
- The framework relies on segment‑level segmentation; for prompts lacking clear sentence/paragraph boundaries, manual or LLM‑driven segmentation may be needed.  
- Experiments were conducted only on GPT‑4.1 mini; cross‑model generalizability beyond this LLM is not empirically verified.  
- Robustness to noisy or weak metrics was evaluated only on SQuAD and GSM8K; extreme noise or very weak supervision may still affect attribution quality.  
- The study does not explore extremely large prompts (> 5,000 tokens) or real‑time latency constraints beyond the reported reductions.
