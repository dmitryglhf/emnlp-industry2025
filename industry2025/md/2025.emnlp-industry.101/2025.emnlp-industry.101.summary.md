# Summary

## TL;DR  
The paper identifies *Format Inertia*—a failure mechanism where LLMs, fine‑tuned on skewed turn‑count data, generate repetitive, diagnostically uninformative questions in long medical pre‑consultations. By rebalancing the training data into a *Uniform Turn‑Count Dataset*, the authors show that both format adherence (FCSR) and clinical utility (TCSR) improve, demonstrating that data distribution matters more than sheer volume for multi‑turn medical dialogue generation.

## Problem & Motivation  
Medical pre‑consultation requires multi‑turn dialogues that progressively gather diagnostic information. Existing SFT approaches train on real‑world data that is heavily skewed toward short conversations, leaving long dialogues under‑represented. This imbalance causes LLMs to fall back on superficial, repetitive question patterns—*Format Inertia*—which preserves format but fails to acquire new diagnostic content. The paper argues that this shortcoming limits the practical deployment of LLMs in healthcare chatbots.

## Approach  
1. **Task Definition**: Generate contextually appropriate questions under two constraint sets—*format constraints* (response format, language, forbidden words, number of options, sentence start/end) and *task constraints* (clinical utility).  
2. **Format Inertia Analysis**: Empirically observe that skewed turn‑count data leads to repetitive questioning, measured via increasing Jaccard and Cosine similarity across turns (Figure 2).  
3. **Uniform Turn‑Count Dataset**:  
   - Bin all dialogues by maximum turn‑count.  
   - Set a quota `q` equal to the smallest bin size.  
   - Sample `q` dialogues from each bin above a minimum threshold `T_min`.  
   - Aggregate samples to form a balanced dataset (Uniform Turn‑Count Dataset).  
   This data‑centric remedy exposes the model to a wide spectrum
