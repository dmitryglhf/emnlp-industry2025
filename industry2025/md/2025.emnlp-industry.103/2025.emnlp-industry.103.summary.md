# Summary

## TL;DR  
The paper adapts the SciNCL contrastive‑learning framework to the process‑industry domain by leveraging graph embeddings (GE) derived from a heterogeneous knowledge graph of text logs and functional locations. Fine‑tuning a small multilingual BERT (mBERT) with GE‑generated triplets yields a 14.3 % MAP@10 improvement over the state‑of‑the‑art mE5‑large encoder while using only 179 M parameters (≈ 3× fewer than mE5‑large’s 560 M). The approach is cost‑efficient and suitable for low‑resource, German‑language industrial settings.

## Problem & Motivation  
Process‑industry plants generate vast amounts of daily operation logs that contain critical technical terminology, abbreviations, and jargon (e.g., “FL 1‑1”, “Lömi”). Existing domain‑adaptation methods (fine‑tuning, DAPT, or training from scratch) rely on abundant unlabeled text, which is scarce in proprietary, non‑English domains. Knowledge graphs (KGs) are routinely stored in these plants, yet their potential for enhancing language models remains under‑exploited. The paper therefore investigates whether graph‑aware contrastive learning can inject KG‑derived semantic signals into a language model, improving semantic search in this low‑resource, German‑language setting.

## Approach  
1. **KG Construction** – A heterogeneous graph is built with two node types:  
   * *Text logs* (TL) – short, note‑like documents (8–21 tokens).  
   * *Functional locations* (FL) – hierarchical machinery units.  
   Three directed relations are added: *reports\_about* (TL → FL), *part\_of* (FL → FL), and *related\_to* (TL ↔ TL).  
   Link‑prediction (entity linking + record linking) is applied to densify the graph.

2. **Graph Embedding (GE)** – A node‑embedding model is trained to bring connected nodes closer in the embedding space. Node embeddings are initialized with sentence‑transformer vectors of TLs and FL descriptions, mirroring Asada et al. (2021).

3. **Triplet Sampling** – For each query document \(d^Q\), the k‑nearest neighbors in GE space are selected as positives (k⁺ = 2, c⁺ = 2) and hard negatives (k⁻\_hard = 50, c⁻\_hard = 1). Easy negatives are sampled randomly (c⁻\_easy = 1). The same strategy as SciNCL is used.

4. **Contrastive Objective** – A triplet margin loss (Schroff et al., 2015) is optimized:
   \[
   \mathcal{L}=\max\{\|d^Q-d^+\|_2-\|d^Q-d^-\|_2+\xi,\,0\}
   \]
   where \(\xi\) is the margin. Document vectors are taken from the last hidden layer of the LM (either [CLS] or [CLS] + mean pooling).

5. **Bi‑Encoder Fine‑Tuning** – Multiple‑Negatives Ranking Loss (from BEIR) is used to train a bi‑encoder on the query‑document pairs derived from GE triplets.

## Experimental Setup  
| Component | Pos. pairs | Neg. pairs | Total |
|-----------|------------|------------|--------|
| Domain‑related MS MARCO | 132 K | 2.14 M | 2.27 M |
| Synthetic domain data (plants A‑F) | 9 K | 32 K | 41 K |
| GE‑based query‑doc pairs (plants A, C, D, G) | 20 K | 80 K | 100 K |
| **Total** | 161 K | 2.25 M | 2.41 M |

- **Training data**: 2.41 M pairs (DR‑MM + SID + GET).  
- **Model selection**: GBERT‑base, mBERT‑base, XLM‑RoBERTa‑base, daGBERT (domain‑adapted via continual pre‑training).  
- **Baselines**: SBERT paraphrase‑multilingual‑MiniLM‑L12‑v, msmarco‑distilbert‑multilingual, mE5‑base, mE5‑large, IBM Granite 107 m, 278 m, OpenAI text‑embedding‑3‑large, mGTE‑base, Nomic Embed v2, deepset‑mxbai‑embed‑de‑large‑v1, M3.  
- **Hardware**: NVIDIA Tesla A100 (80 GB).  
- **Optimization**: Adam with weight decay, learning rate \(2^{-5}\).  
- **Training schedule**: 3 epochs (GBERT, daGBERT, mBERT) or 5/8 epochs (XLM‑RoBERTa). Batch sizes: 16 (LM fine‑tuning), 64 (bi‑encoder).  
- **Evaluation**: PITEB – 330 K documents, 205 queries, 14 680 relevant docs (7 plants, 4 unseen during training). Metrics: MAP@10, MRR@10, nDCG@10, Mean.

**Key findings**  
- Fine‑tuned mBERT with GE triplets achieves a MAP@10 of **59.82 %**, surpassing the state‑of‑the‑art mE5‑large (59.82 % vs 59.82 %? Actually the paper claims a 14.3 % improvement over mE5‑large, i.e., 7.96 p points).  
- The same model outperforms the best baseline M3 by **1.5 % (0.92 p)**.  
- The fine‑tuned model uses **179 M parameters**, roughly **3× fewer** than mE5‑large’s 560 M.  
- Training required **2.45 M text pairs** (2.41 M total) and **3 epochs**.
