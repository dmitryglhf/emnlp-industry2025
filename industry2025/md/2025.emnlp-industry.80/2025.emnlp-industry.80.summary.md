# Summary

## TL;DR
Auto‑Weighted Group Relative Policy Optimization (AW‑GRPO) automatically tunes reward weights during training, preventing reward hacking and over‑fitting that plague the original GRPO.  On two real‑world tasks—English‑to‑Japanese simplification and internal advertising text generation—AW‑GRPO matches the base model’s BLEURT while improving readability, diversity, and constraint compliance, achieving the best overall scores among all baselines.

## Problem & Motivation
Many industrial NLP applications require simultaneously optimizing several objectives (e.g., semantic fidelity, readability, diversity) while obeying hard constraints (length, brackets, symbol limits).  Existing GRPO fixes reward weights before training, which forces the policy to focus on the easiest reward and can lead to reward hacking or over‑fitting.  The paper targets this shortcoming in two concrete settings: (1) WMT‑En‑Ja translation with a readability objective, and (2) company‑internal advertising text generation that must satisfy multiple structural constraints.

## Approach
AW‑GRPO extends GRPO by dynamically rescaling the reward coefficients \(\alpha_k\) during training.  
1. **Progress estimation** – For each reward \(R_k\), the slope \(\hat{s}_k^{(t)}\) of a least‑squares polynomial fitted to the last \(n\) reward values is computed.  
2. **Weight update** –  
   \[
   w_k^{(t+1)} = \text{clip}\!\bigl(\alpha_k^{(t)}\,e^{-\eta\,\hat{s}_k^{(t)}},\,w_{\min},\,w_{\max}\bigr)
   \]  
   followed by normalisation  
   \[
   \alpha_k^{(t+1)} = \frac{w_k^{(t+1)}}{\sum_i w_i^{(t+1)}}
   \]  
   (Eq. 4‑5).  
3. **Reward aggregation** – The updated \(\alpha_k^{(t+1)}\) are used in the GRPO objective (Eq. 3).  
The exponential decay penalises rewards that improve too quickly, while rewards that lag receive higher weight, thus balancing learning progress across all objectives.

## Experimental Setup
| Task | Dataset | Base Model | Rewards | Constraints | Evaluation Metrics |
|------|---------|------------|---------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Machine Translation** | WMT‑21/22/23 En‑Ja (train) + WMT‑24 En‑Ja (test) | Sarashina (sarashina2.2‑3b‑instruct‑v0.1) | BLEURT, jReadability | None | BLEURT, jReadability, GPT‑4o‑mini win ratio |
| **Advertising Text** | Internal: 8,400 train, 500 test | Qwen (Qwen3‑4B) | BLEU, BLEURT, diversity (1‑cosine) | Length, Bracket, Num‑Limit, Auxiliary (via validators) | BLEU, BLEURT, Dist‑2, jReadability, constraint violations, 5‑Sent |

Baselines: Base Model (no RL), GRPO (fixed \(\alpha_k=1\)), AW‑GRPO (dynamic \(\alpha_k\)).  Hyper‑parameters \(\eta, w_{\min}, w_{\max}\) are detailed in Appendix F.

## Results
### Machine Translation (Table 2)
| Method | BLEURT↑ | jReadability↑ | GPT‑4(Win Ratio)↑ |
|--------|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Base Model | 0.66 | 0.70 | N/A |
| GRPO | 0.63 ± 0.005 | 0.86 ± 0.008 | 25.5 ± 1.81 % |
| AW‑GRPO | 0.66 ± 0 | 0.80 ± 0.01 | 74.5 ± 1.86 % |

AW‑GRPO preserves the BLEURT of the base model while substantially improving jReadability and win ratio.  Table 3 shows the average weights during training: BLEURT = 0.69 ± 0.01, jReadability = 0.31 ± 0.01, indicating that AW‑GRPO increases the weight of BLEURT to counteract its over‑fitting.

### Advertising Text (Table 4)
| Variant | Length | Bracket | Num Limit | Auxiliary | 5‑Sent | Dist‑2 | jReadability |
|---------|--------|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|--------|--------------|
| GRPO w/o Validator | N/A | N/A | N/A | N/A | 500 ± 0 | – | N/A |
| GRPO w/ Validator | 50 ± 2 | 2 ± 1 | 0 ± 0 | 0 ± 0 | 0 ± 0 | – | 0.81 ± 0.00 |
| AW‑GRPO w/o Validator | 153 ± 5 | 13 ± 2 | 0 ± 0 | 0 ± 0 | 0 ± 0 | 0.69 ± 0.02 | 0.81 ± 0.00 |
| AW‑GRPO w/ Validator | **34 ± 2** | **0 ± 0** | 0 ± 0 | 0 ± 0 | 0 ± 0 | 0.69 ± 0.02 | 0.81 ± 0.00 |

Key observations:
* AW‑GRPO with the validator reduces length violations from 153 to **34** and eliminates all bracket‑mismatch errors (from 13 to **0**).  
* Constraint violations for Num‑Limit and Auxiliary remain zero in all cases.  
* Dist‑2 and jReadability are unchanged, but the validator’s presence is essential for compliance.  
* GRPO without the validator fails to produce five outputs; with the validator it still suffers from reward hacking (high BLEU/BLEURT but low constraint compliance).  

Overall, AW‑GRPO achieves the best formatting compliance and maintains high BLEU/BLEURT scores.
