# Summary

## TL;DR
The paper demonstrates how to compress a 100‑B parameter internal foundation model (FM) for LinkedIn recommendation systems into a 20× smaller model while keeping AUC and validation loss essentially unchanged.  Knowledge distillation (KD) followed by structured pruning (MLP and attention head removal) and optional FP8 quantization yields latency‑friendly, high‑throughput SLMs that can be served online in production.

## Problem & Motivation
Large language models (LLMs) deliver state‑of‑the‑art performance on recommendation tasks but are too heavy for latency‑sensitive production workloads.  The authors need a practical pipeline that turns a 100‑B FM into a deployable model for LinkedIn’s recommendation systems (ranking, recommendation, reasoning) without sacrificing accuracy.

## Approach
1. **Knowledge Distillation** – Train a student from the FM using a weighted loss: 90 % forward KL between teacher and student logits, 10 % cross‑entropy with ground‑truth labels, and an extra 5 % loss over the entire prompt (normalized by token count).  
2. **Structured Pruning** – Apply OSS‑CAR to prune MLP up/down‑projection neurons and attention heads in a layer‑wise fashion.  One‑shot pruning is followed by a fine‑tuning step (SFT or KD) to recover any lost generalization.  
3. **Quantization** – Optional FP8 quantization is applied post‑pruning to further reduce memory and compute.  
4. **Two‑Stage Training for Reasoning** – For reasoning tasks, a 1.5B student is first distilled from a Qwen‑2.5‑1.5B‑Instruct teacher (3B, 7B, or 14B).  A second on‑policy stage (oFKL) with a sampling fraction of 1.0 and token generation length of 300 tokens yields the best validation loss.

## Experimental Setup
- **Datasets** – Internal LinkedIn recommendation tasks (text‑only featurization), OpenThoughts reasoning dataset, AIME 2024/2025 benchmarks.  
- **Baselines** – SFT only, various KD losses (FKL, JSD, RKL, oFKL), and different teacher sizes (3B, 7B, 14B).  
- **Metrics** – AUC for predictive tasks; validation loss for reasoning tasks.  
- **Models** – FM (100B, MoE, 16 experts, 4 active per token), distilled students (8B, 6.8B, 6.4B, 6.0B, 3B, 2.4B, 2.1B).  
- **Deployment** – Online serving on LinkedIn’s recommendation platform; latency‑sensitive use cases.

## Results
| Model | #Params | AUC Δ % | Validation Loss |
|-------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| 8B FM | 8 B | – | – |
| 6.8B Pruned | 6.8 B | 0.0 | – |
| 6.4B Pruned | 6.4 B | –0.47 % (SFT) / –0.06 % (KD) | – |
| 6.0B Pruned | 6.0 B | –1.72 | – |
| 3B Distilled | 3 B | – | – |
| 2.4B Pruned | 2.4 B | –0.12 % (one‑shot) / +0.03 % (gradual) | – |
| 2.1B Attention Pruned | 2.1 B | –1.07 % (SFT) / +0.02 % (KD) | – |

**Reasoning Task (1.5B student)** – Validation loss (lower better):

| Teacher | Training | Val Loss |
|---------|----------|----------|
| 3B | FKL | 0.2015 |
| 7B | FKL | 0.1939 |
| 14B | FKL | 0.1863 |
| 3B → 1.5B | FKL‑oFKL (tk=300) | 0.1894 |
| 7B → 1.5B | FKL‑oFKL (tk=300) | 0.1894 |
| 14B → 1.5B | FKL‑oFKL (tk=300) | 0.1863 |

Key observations:
- KD consistently outperforms SFT (e.g., 8B‑KD Δ –0.06 % vs 8B‑SFT Δ –0.62 %).
- One‑shot pruning followed by KD recovers most of the lost AUC; gradual pruning yields near‑lossless compression.
- Attention‑head pruning alone hurts AUC (–1.07 %); adding KD restores performance (+0.02 %).
- For reasoning, a two‑stage training (FKL + oFKL) gives the lowest validation loss; larger teachers (14B) provide marginal gains.

## Industry Impact
The compressed models are deployed online at LinkedIn’s professional social network platform.  The 20× size reduction allows the FM‑derived SLMs to be served with modest latency, enabling real‑time ranking, recommendation, and reasoning in production recommendation systems.  The paper reports that the resulting models meet throughput and latency constraints for high‑scale recommendation workloads.
