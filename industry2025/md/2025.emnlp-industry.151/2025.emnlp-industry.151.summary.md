# Summary

## TL;DR  
DORIS is a hybrid depression‑detection framework that fuses Large Language Models (LLMs) for clinically‑grounded feature extraction with a Gradient Boosting Tree (GBT) classifier for robust prediction.  On the SWDD dataset it outperforms all baselines, achieving an AUPRC of **0.8147** and a precision of **0.7606** while still providing LLM‑generated explanations that map DSM‑5 symptoms and mood trajectories to the final decision.

## Problem & Motivation  
Limited access to mental‑healthcare resources creates a critical treatment gap for depression. Social‑media platforms offer a scalable data source for early detection, but existing methods struggle on two fronts:  
1. **Medical knowledge integration** – they cannot reliably distinguish clinical depression from transient mood changes.  
2. **Explainability** – deep models act as black boxes, lacking the evidence‑based reasoning clinicians require.  
The paper therefore targets a system that can both achieve high accuracy and provide transparent, medically‑relevant explanations.

## Approach  
**Core idea** – a two‑stage pipeline:  
1. **LLM‑based feature generation**  
   * **DSM‑5 symptom annotation** – GPT‑4o‑mini is prompted to label each post with a 9‑dimensional binary vector indicating presence of DSM‑5 symptoms (A–I).  
   * **Mood‑course summarization** – posts with high emotional content (filtered by five emotion templates) are fed to the LLM to produce a narrative description of the user’s longitudinal mood trajectory.  
   * **Post‑history embedding** – all posts are embedded with the low‑resource gte‑small model and averaged.  
2. **GBT classification** – the three feature vectors (diagnostic criteria, mood‑course, post‑history) are concatenated (diagnostic criteria in a separate space) and fed to an XGBoost GBT.  Post‑processing calibration (isotonic regression) is applied to improve probability reliability.

**Notable design choices**  
* Efficient annotation: only posts with top‑k similarity to DSM‑5 templates are sent to the LLM, reducing cost.  
* Low‑compute embedding: gte‑small (1 GB RAM) keeps the system deployable in low‑resource settings.  
* Explainability: the final prediction is supported by the LLM‑generated symptom list and mood‑course narrative, and a second LLM pass produces a justification text that explicitly links these evidences to the classification.

## Experimental Setup  
| Item | Details |
|------|----------|
| **Datasets** | **SWDD** – 1 000 depressed users, 19 000 controls (≈70 k depressed posts, 1.3 M control posts).  **Twitter Mental Disorder** – used for generalizability (details in Appendix B). |
| **Implementation** | Cosine similarity for embeddings.  gte‑small for text embeddings.  GPT‑4o‑mini for LLM calls.  XGBoost for GBT.  5 runs, average reported. |
| **Baselines** | TF‑IDF+XGBoost, HAN, Mood2Content, AMM‑Net, FastText, gte‑small, BERT, MentalRoBERTa, PHQ9 (Score), PHQ9 (Vector), GPT‑4o‑mini, MentalLLama. |
| **Metrics** | Precision, Recall, F1‑score, AUROC, AUPRC. |

## Results  
| Metric | DORIS | Best Baseline |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Precision | **0.7606** | 0.7467 (FastText) |
| Recall | **0.7902** | 0.7703 (PHQ9 (Score)) |
| F1‑score | **0.7750** | 0.7102 (BERT) |
| AUROC | **0.9722** | 0.9499 (gte‑small) |
| AUPRC | **0.8147** | 0.7774 (Mood2Content) |

DORIS achieves a **0.0361** gain in AUPRC over the current best baseline (Mood2Content).  Ablation studies (not shown) confirm each component’s contribution, and case studies illustrate the system’s ability to generate explainable assessments backed by concrete evidence.
