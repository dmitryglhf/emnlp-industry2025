# Summary

## TL;DR  
The paper introduces **Information Provision Activity Requirement Extraction (IPARE)**, a new IE task on EU acquis legislation that identifies obligations to provide information and extracts key entities and temporal details. A benchmark corpus of 1,212 sentence‑level snippets is released, and the authors compare knowledge‑based, classical ML, transformer, and generative‑AI approaches. Generative LLMs (especially Claude‑3.5‑Sonnet) achieve the best detection and extraction performance, demonstrating the feasibility of AI‑driven compliance tooling for industry.

## Problem & Motivation  
EU law is stored in *Cellar* but lacks metadata for information‑provision obligations, making it hard for companies and administrations to rationalise compliance. Existing NLP work focuses on case law or simple classification; no IE system exists for EU acquis. The paper addresses this gap by defining IPARE and providing a practical solution that can be deployed in real‑world legal‑tech pipelines.

## Approach  
- **Task definition**: Two subtasks – (1) binary detection of a provision activity requirement, (2) template‑filling extraction of *providing entity*, *target entity*, *deadline*, and *frequency*.  
- **Detection models**:  
  - *Baseline‑K* (keyword presence) and *Baseline‑KD* (keyword + temporal expression).  
  - Classical ML: Logistic Regression (LR) and linear SVM (SVM) with character‑n‑gram features.  
  - Transformers: XLMRoBERTa, Legal‑RoBERTa, EU‑BERT (classification head, frozen base layers).  
  - Generative LLMs: LLama‑3.2‑3B, Mistral‑7B, Mistral‑24B, Mistral‑Large (24.02), GPT‑4o, Claude‑3.5‑Sonnet (zero‑shot and few‑shot, n=10).  
- **Extraction models**:  
  - *Knowledge‑based (KB)*: 2,500 lexical resources + 45 high‑level lexico‑syntactic patterns (ExPRESS).  
  - *KB+* variant allowing longer token spans for higher recall.  
  - *LLM‑based*: few‑shot and Chain‑of‑Thought prompts, generating a JSON dictionary; Claude used function‑calling to enforce schema.  
  - *Hybrid*: KB first, then LLM to fill missing slots.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Dataset**: 1,212 EU‑acquis snippets (≈262 chars avg). 55 % positive; 91.8 % providing entity, 86.9 % target entity, 67.2 % deadline, 57.7 % frequency.  
- **Data source**: 110 K EU‑acquis documents chunked into sentences; 973 sampled via keyword/temporal criteria; 239 additional sentences from JRC‑RO (Daudaravicius & Friis‑Christensen 2024).  
- **Cross‑validation**: 5‑fold, 80 % train / 20 % test per fold.  
- **Metrics**: Detection – precision, recall, F1, accuracy. Extraction – precision, recall, F1 (exact and fuzzy, fuzzy uses 0.75 LCS similarity).  
- **Evaluation**: Table 1 (not reproduced here) shows baseline F1 ≈ 0.80; SVM 0.927; Legal‑RoBERTa 0.90; Claude‑3.5‑Sonnet zero‑shot 0.92; few‑shot 0.949. Extraction: Claude‑3.5‑Sonnet highest F1 for providing/target entities and deadline.

## Results  
- **Detection**: Classical ML (SVM) outperforms transformers; best transformer Legal‑RoBERTa 0.90. Generative LLMs lead: Claude‑3.5‑Sonnet 0.949 (few‑shot).  
- **Extraction**: Claude‑3.5‑Sonnet achieves highest F1 on providing/target entities and deadline; function‑calling improves JSON compliance.  
- **Statistical spread**: Standard deviation across folds 0.006–0.047 (Table 3, Appendix C).
