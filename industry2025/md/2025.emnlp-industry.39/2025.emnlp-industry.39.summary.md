# Summary

## TL;DR  
SFAL introduces a lightweight, LLM‑free metric for evaluating auto‑interpretations in sparse autoencoders (SAEs). By aligning semantic embeddings of feature explanations with functional co‑occurrence patterns, it achieves comparable or better correlation with human judgments than the state‑of‑the‑art fuzzy scoring while cutting computational cost by orders of magnitude, enabling large‑scale deployment in real‑world LLM systems.

## Problem & Motivation  
Interpreting the internal representations of large language models (LLMs) is essential for safety, debugging, and compliance. Existing evaluation methods for auto‑interpretability rely heavily on expensive LLM‑based scoring (e.g., fuzzy, detection, surprisal, embedding scoring) and suffer from high computational cost, noise, and poor scalability to millions of SAE features. The paper targets this gap, aiming to provide a cost‑effective, scalable alternative that can be used in industry deployments.

## Approach  
SFAL quantifies the alignment between a feature’s semantic neighbourhood (derived from auto‑interpretation embeddings) and its functional neighbourhood (derived from co‑occurrence statistics).  
- **Semantic representation**: Each SAE feature \(s_i\) is encoded into a vector \(\mathbf{a}_i\) using a language‑model encoder (selected from top performers on the MTEB leaderboard).  
- **Functional representation**: Co‑occurrence counts between feature pairs are collected into a \(2\times2\) contingency table, from which the phi coefficient \(\phi_{ij}\) is computed.  
- **Neighbourhoods**: For each feature, the top‑\(K_S\) semantic neighbours (highest cosine similarity) and top‑\(K_F\) functional neighbours (highest positive \(\phi_{ij}\)) are selected.  
- **SFAL score**: The Normalised Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) between the two ranked lists is computed, yielding a score in \([0,1]\).  
Design choices: fixed top‑\(K\) neighbourhoods for reproducibility, use of phi coefficient (well‑suited for binary co‑occurrence), and NDCG (well‑established for ranking consistency). The method is deliberately LLM‑free, relying only on encoder embeddings and co‑occurrence statistics.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Datasets**: 50 k documents from the training corpora of gemma‑2‑9b and llama‑3.1‑8b; chunk size 256 tokens.  
- **Models**: 16k‑feature SAE for gemma‑2‑9b and 32k‑feature SAE for llama‑3.1‑8b. Five layers were evaluated: 0, 8, 17, 25, and final layer (41 for gemma‑2‑9b, 31 for llama‑3.1‑8b).  
- **Baselines**: fuzzy score (Paulo et al., 2024) computed with Gemini‑2.0‑flash (≈ $100 API cost).  
- **Evaluation metrics**: Pearson, Spearman, and Kendall correlations between automated scores and human judgments.  
- **Human study**: 4 expert users rated 100 sampled auto‑interpretations (20 per layer) on soundness and completeness (1–4 Likert). Median confidence 3, Krippendorff’s α = 0.64 (gemma) and 0.57 (llama).  
- **Computational resources**: 2 GPU‑hours on a single NVIDIA A100 (SFAL) versus $100 API cost for fuzzy.

## Results  
| Metric | Gemma‑2‑9b | | | Llama‑3.1‑8b | | | |
|--------|------------|---|---|--------------|---|---|
| | Pearson | Spearman | Kendall | Pearson | Spearman | Kendall |
| **Fuzz score** (Paulo et al., 2024) | 0.47 | 0.56 | 0.40 | 0.59 | 0.60 | 0.44 |
| **SFAL Bilingual Emb** (Thakur et al., 2020) | 0.63 | 0.62 | 0.45 | 0.53 | 0.56 | 0.41 |
| **SFAL gte‑Qwen2‑7B‑instruct** (Li et al., 2023) | 0.53 | 0.50 | 0.37 | 0.48 | 0.53 | 0.39 |
| **SFAL Qwen3‑Emb‑8B** (prompted) (Zhang et al., 2025) | 0.66 | 0.63 | 0.46 | – | – | – |

- SFAL consistently shows stronger positive correlation with human judgments than the fuzzy baseline for Gemma‑2‑9b across all embedding models.  
- For Llama‑3.1‑8b, SFAL slightly underperforms the fuzzy score (Pearson 0.53 vs 0.59).  
- SFAL scores are generally lower than fuzzy, indicating a more selective assessment.  
- The method required only 2 GPU‑hours on an A100, whereas fuzzy incurred ≈ $100 in API fees.

## Industry Impact  
Not reported.

## Limitations  
- SFAL underperforms fuzzy scoring on the Llama‑3.1‑8b dataset.  
- Experiments were limited to 50 k documents, 5 layers, and 4 expert raters.  
- The method relies on fixed top‑\(K\) neighbourhoods; adaptive thresholds were not explored.  
- Only a single GPU (A100) was used; scalability to larger corpora or feature sets was not empirically tested beyond the reported 16k/32k feature SAEs.  
- Correlation analysis was performed only on Gemma‑2‑9b and Llama‑3.1‑8b; generalisation to other models remains unverified.
