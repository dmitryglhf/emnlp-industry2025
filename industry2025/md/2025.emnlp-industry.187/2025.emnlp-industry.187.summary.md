# Summary

## TL;DR  
GEAR is a fully automated, reference‑based evaluation framework for retrieval‑augmented generation (RAG) car assistants. It uses LLMs to compare assistant responses against automatically generated pseudo ground‑truth (PGT) answers, decomposing both into key facts labeled essential, optional, or safety‑critical. The framework reports multiple fine‑grained metrics—completeness (soft/hard), risk awareness, correctness, relevance, confidence, and actionability—showing close alignment with human judgments and enabling scalable, industry‑ready evaluation of automotive QA systems.

## Problem & Motivation  
Modern vehicles increasingly embed AI‑powered assistants that answer maintenance, troubleshooting, and operational queries. Existing evaluation metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR) rely on surface‑level overlap and fail to capture factual accuracy, completeness, and safety awareness—critical for automotive contexts. The lack of a human‑aligned, scalable evaluation method hampers reliable deployment of RAG‑based car assistants. GEAR addresses this gap by providing a reference‑based, fact‑level assessment tailored to automotive QA.

## Approach  
GEAR consists of three LLM‑driven modules:

1. **Test Set Generator** – Generates high‑quality pseudo ground‑truth QA pairs from the Tesla Model 3 2024 manual.  
   * Text‑based QA: GPT‑4o produces non‑overlapping questions per manual section, then retrieves the two most semantically relevant chunks (Sentence‑BERT, cosine similarity) and prompts GPT‑4o to generate answers.  
   * Image‑based QA: For sections containing images, GPT‑4o receives the image and associated text and generates QA pairs grounded in visual content.

2. **SplitTag** – Decomposes both PGT answers and assistant responses into atomic key facts using GPT‑4o. Each fact is tagged as **Essential** (directly answers the question), **Optional** (adds context but not required), or **Safety‑Critical** (communicates a safety precaution, warning, or risk).

3. **Evaluator** – Compares the assistant’s key facts against the PGT facts using LLM prompts. It computes the following metrics:  
   * **Completeness‑Hard** – proportion of all PGT facts covered.  
   * **Completeness‑Soft** – proportion of essential PGT facts covered.  
   * **Risk Awareness** – proportion of safety‑critical PGT facts addressed.  
   * **Correctness** – proportion of relevant assistant facts that are factually accurate.  
   * **Relevance** – proportion of assistant facts that directly address the user’s question.  
   * **Confidence** – binary indicator of clear vs. vague language.  
   * **Actionability** – binary indicator of concrete actionable steps.

The framework employs GPT‑4o, Gemini, and DeepSeek as evaluators to examine consistency and evaluator bias.

## Experimental Setup  
| Item | Details |
|-------|----------|
| **Dataset** | Tesla Model 3 2024 manual |
| **PGT QA pairs** | 3,202 total (2,967 text‑based, 235 image‑based) |
| **Evaluation subset** | 716 text‑based + 38 image‑based QA pairs (sampled one per semantic section) |
| **RAG assistant** | Three LLMs (GPT‑4o, Gemini, DeepSeek) with identical preprocessing, chunking, embedding, and retrieval |
| **Chunking** | Fixed‑size sliding window, 128 tokens / 32 tokens overlap |
| **Embedding** | Sentence‑BERT, 768‑dimensional |
| **Retrieval engine** | FAISS (cosine similarity) |
| **Top‑k chunks** | 3 |
| **Evaluation metrics** | Completeness‑Hard, Completeness‑Soft, Risk Awareness, Correctness, Relevance, Confidence, Actionability |
| **Human annotation** | 10 % of full pool (75 QAs) for key‑fact extraction and tagging |

## Results  
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Completeness (soft)** | 80.2 % |
| **Relevance** | 85.2 % |
| **Correctness** | 91.2 % |
| **Essentiality F1 (SplitTag)** | 89.2 % |
| **Safety‑Critical F1 (SplitTag)** | 80.2 % |

The Test Set Generator achieved > 80 % alignment with human‑labeled key facts. SplitTag matched human essentiality and safety‑critical tagging with F1 scores of 89.2 % and 80.2 % respectively, though it exhibited lower recall for safety‑critical facts, indicating a less conservative stance than human annotators. GEAR’s metrics closely matched human judgments, validating its reliability for large‑scale evaluation.

## Limitations  
* SplitTag’s lower recall for safety‑critical labeling suggests it may miss some safety concerns compared to human experts.  
* The framework relies on LLM prompts; evaluator bias and prompt sensitivity are potential issues, as noted in the related‑work discussion.  
* No deployment or A/B‑test results are provided, limiting insight into real‑world performance gains.
