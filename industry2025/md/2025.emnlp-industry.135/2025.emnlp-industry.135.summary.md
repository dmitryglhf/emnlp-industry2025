# Summary

## TL;DR  
Airbnb’s *Agent‑in‑the‑Loop* (AITL) framework turns live customer‑support interactions into a continuous data flywheel for LLM‑based RAG systems. By collecting four types of human annotations—pairwise response preference, adoption rationale, knowledge relevance, and missing knowledge—AITL reduces model‑update cycles from months to weeks and yields a 14.8 % lift in precision@8, 11.7 % lift in recall@75, 8.4 % boost in helpfulness, and a 4.5 % rise in agent adoption. The approach is industry‑track relevant because it demonstrates a production‑ready, low‑latency feedback loop that directly improves a commercial customer‑support product.

## Problem & Motivation  
Customer‑support LLMs must stay current with evolving product features, policies, and user expectations. Traditional offline pipelines rely on batch annotations and static knowledge cut‑offs, leading to drift and stale responses. Existing RLHF or Arena‑Learning methods either lack domain‑specific knowledge retrieval or do not embed real‑time human feedback. Airbnb needed a system that could continuously ingest live interaction data, adapt retrieval and generation modules, and maintain high performance in a real‑world asynchronous messaging channel.

## Approach  
1. **Unified Knowledge Base** – Consolidates guides, FAQs, internal policies, workflows, dynamic context (e.g., reservation status), and historical cases into a single, metadata‑rich repository.  
2. **Agent Annotation Interface** – During live conversations agents evaluate two suggested responses and provide:  
   * Pairwise preference (significantly better / better / slightly better).  
   * Adoption decision and free‑text rationale.  
   * Relevance score of the knowledge used by the LLM.  
   * Missing‑knowledge flag via a dedicated selection interface.  
3. **Review Annotation** – A human expert and an LLM‑based virtual judge (VJ) jointly review agent annotations to detect inconsistencies.  
4. **Continuous Learning Pipeline** –  
   * **Data aggregation & filtering**: rule‑based thresholds + LLM‑based VJ filtering for prompt adherence.  
   * **Model retraining**: Retrieval, ranking, and generation modules are fine‑tuned with LoRA/QLoRA on Ray clusters (GLOW modules).  
   * **Evaluation**: Batch inference on curated datasets, using ground‑truth and virtual judges.  
   * **Deployment**: Updated models are rolled back into the RAG system, closing the loop.

## Experimental Setup  
* **Baseline** – Offline workflow: logs + simulation data → preference/adoption annotations via spreadsheets, knowledge relevance via Labelbox, validated by human review. Took 3 months to train.  
* **AITL Pilot** – 40 US‑based agents in an asynchronous messaging channel (SLA: responses within hours). Collected >5,000 cases; each agent annotated ~11 cases daily.  
* **Datasets & Metrics** – Retrieval: recall@75, precision@8. Generation: helpfulness (point‑wise & pair‑wise), citation correctness (Jaccard overlap), response correctness.  
* **Baselines** – Baseline (no fine‑tuning), Offline Data Fine‑tuned (offline annotations + ORPO), AITL Fine‑tuned (AITL annotations + ORPO).  

## Results  
| Retrieval | Baseline | Offline Fine‑tuned | AITL Fine‑tuned |
|-----------|----------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Recall@75 | 0.634 | 0.670 | **0.708** (+11.7 %) |
| Precision@8 | 0.357 | 0.394 | **0.410** (+14.8 %) |

| Generation | Baseline | Offline Fine‑tuned | AITL Fine‑tuned |
|------------|----------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Helpfulness | 0.658 | 0.691 | **0.713** (+8.4 %) |
| Citation | 0.097 | 0.112 | **0.134** (+38.1 %) |
| Response Correctness | 0.851 | 0.868 | **0.882** (+3.6 %) |

*Human Preference*: 60.12 % of AITL responses preferred over baseline (vs. 33.32 % baseline), with 6.57 % neutral.  
*Adoption Rate*: ↑ 4.5 % relative to baseline.  
*Annotation Agreement*: Online vs offline – preference 0.832 vs 0.635, adoption 0.775 vs 0.721, relevance 0.923 vs 0.436.  
*Immediate vs Delayed Annotation*: Immediate improves missing‑knowledge agreement from 63.9 % to 76.5 % (+12 pp).  
*LLM‑based Filter Ablation*: Retrieval recall@75 0.708 vs 0.670, precision@8 0.402 vs 0.394; citation 0.131 vs 0.112.  

## Industry Impact  
AITL was deployed at Airbnb in a production customer‑support product used by 40 US agents. The system ran in real‑time, requiring no interruption to SLA. The continuous data flywheel enabled weekly model updates (vs. months), directly improving the product’s retrieval accuracy, response helpfulness, and agent adoption. The pilot demonstrated that embedding human feedback into operational workflows yields measurable business gains in a commercial setting.

## Limitations  
* Immediate annotation is only feasible when SLAs allow brief delays; for stricter channels (e.g., live‑chat) a hybrid workflow is recommended.  
* Missing‑knowledge step benefits uniquely from immediate human annotation; other steps show negligible timing effects.  
* LLM‑based filtering is a noise gate; it does not generate missing‑knowledge labels and may miss nuanced domain knowledge.  
* Offline evaluation of Step 4 (missing knowledge) was not performed due to tool limitations.  
* The approach relies on a large, well‑structured knowledge base; performance may degrade if the base is incomplete or poorly metadata‑tagged.  
* The study was limited to an asynchronous messaging channel; generalization to other modalities remains to be validated.
