# Summary

## TL;DR  
PatternRAG is a retrieval‑augmented generation system that exploits product catalog patterns to predict missing structured attributes in multilingual e‑commerce stores. By combining term‑based filtering, BM25 title similarity, brand‑ and glance‑view reranking, and few‑shot examples, it boosts recall by up to **34 %** and entry‑level completeness by up to **43 %** over a plain LLM prompt, demonstrating tangible value for large‑scale catalog quality improvement.

## Problem & Motivation  
E‑commerce catalogs contain millions of product entries, each with unstructured attributes (titles, descriptions) and structured attributes (SAs) such as *color* or *material*. In practice, roughly half of the relevant SAs are missing, leading to incomplete listings that hurt discovery and sales. Existing solutions rely on generic prompt engineering or external retrieval, but LLMs perform unevenly across languages and cannot always infer missing values from titles alone. The paper targets this real‑world issue in Amazon’s multilingual stores (US, DE, FR), where consistent catalog patterns exist but are underutilized.

## Approach  
PatternRAG’s core idea is to treat the catalog itself as a knowledge base. The system operates in five stages:

1. **Offline index building** – language‑specific search indices (SI) are created from all high‑quality catalog entries.  
2. **Term‑based filtering** – the product type (PT) of the query entry restricts the search space to the same PT.  
3. **BM25 title similarity** – Lucene 8.11’s OKAPI BM25 ranks the top‑k (k = 100) similar titles within the PT.  
4. **Heuristic reranking** – the ranked list is first sorted by *glance views* (GV) and then brand‑aligned (entries from the same brand are moved to the front).  
5. **Few‑shot example construction** – for each missing SA, up to three entries with a filled value are selected; each example consists of the product title and the attribute‑value pair. These examples are injected into an attribute‑specific prompt that instructs the LLM to use them as references.

The LLM used is **Mixtral‑8x7B‑Instruct**. The prompt hierarchy is:  
- **Base Prompt** – includes all UAs, SAs, and external knowledge.  
- **Base Prompt + PatternRAG** – adds the few‑shot examples and an explicit instruction to use them.

Design choices: BM25 was chosen for its surface‑level similarity and robustness to multilingual noise; brand and GV reranking enforce consistency with catalog conventions; few‑shot examples provide concrete, context‑rich guidance to the LLM.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Index corpus**: 30 M entries for the US store, 20 M for DE and FR.  
- **Test dataset**: 3 000 catalog entries per store, sampled across product types; ~50 % of SAs are missing.  
- **SA relevance tags**: each SA is labeled *VRel* (very relevant) or *Rel* (relevant) based on business importance.  
- **Baselines**: *Base Prompt* (plain LLM prompt) vs *Base Prompt + PatternRAG*.  
- **Evaluation metrics**:  
  - Attribute‑level precision, recall, F1.  
  - Catalog‑entry‑level *completeness* and *correctness* (defined via thresholds on VRel SAs).  
- **Implementation**: Retrieval via Lucene 8.11; LLM via Mixtral‑8x7B‑Instruct.  
- **Deployment context**: offline evaluation on the test sets; no online A/B‑test reported.

## Industry Impact  
PatternRAG was deployed in Amazon’s e‑commerce stores: US, DE, and FR. The system operates at scale (30 M US entries, 20 M DE/FR entries) and directly improves catalog data quality—higher completeness and correctness—leading to better product discovery and a smoother customer experience. The paper reports no explicit revenue lift but indicates tangible business value through improved catalog metrics.

## Limitations  
- Recall improvements vary across stores; US gains are modest compared to FR.  
- Few‑shot example coverage is limited (33–43 % of empty SAs).  
- The study is offline; no online A/B‑test results are reported.  
- Computational overhead of multi‑stage retrieval and prompt construction is not quantified.  
- The approach assumes the existence of high‑quality catalog entries for indexing; noisy or mis‑labelled entries may affect retrieval quality.
