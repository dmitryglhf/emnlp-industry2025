# Summary

## TL;DR  
The paper introduces the **Structural Reward Model (SRM)**, a modular reward‑modeling framework that augments traditional scalar reward models with **side‑branch models (SBMs)** to generate auxiliary features. By concatenating these features with prompt–response pairs and training a Bradley‑Terry pairwise RM, SRM achieves higher alignment with human preferences, improved interpretability, and faster inference than both scalar and generative reward models. Experiments on RM‑Bench, JudgeBench, and the new IFBENCH benchmark show substantial gains across a range of base models, including Llama3‑8B‑Instruct, internlm2‑7B‑Reward, and internlm2‑20B‑Reward.

## Problem & Motivation  
Scalar reward models (RMs) only use the prompt and generated output, missing richer contextual and background information, which leads to incomplete evaluations. Generative RMs (GRMs) generate intermediate reasoning but are black‑box, inefficient (sequential decoding), and hard to interpret, especially in industrial settings such as search or recommendation systems where single‑domain tasks require evaluation along specific dimensions (relevance, timeliness, authority). Structured feedback is needed to diagnose “bad cases” and optimize dimension‑specific issues. Thus, existing RMs fall short in interpretability, efficiency, and scalability for industry applications.

## Approach  
SRM augments the reward‑modeling pipeline with **five side‑branch models** (SBMs) based on LLaMA3‑8B and fine‑tuned with LoRA:

1. **SB‑Semantic** – extracts deep semantic structure from the prompt–response pair.  
2. **SB‑Entity** – expands entity background using external knowledge graphs.  
3. **SB‑FactCheck** – verifies factual consistency and outputs an accuracy analysis.  
4. **SB‑Style** – analyzes style, tone, and wording, comparing with the prompt.  
5. **SB‑Quality** – evaluates diversity and creativity to avoid repetitive content.

Training data for SBMs is generated via a **Best‑of‑N sampling** strategy, then filtered by an **LLM‑as‑a‑judge** (LLaMa3‑8B “o1”) with a quality threshold τ. The resulting high‑quality triples \((p, r, a^{(i)})\) are used to fine‑tune each SBM with a maximum‑likelihood objective (Eq. 4).

During inference, each SBM produces auxiliary text \(t_c^{(i)}\) (for the chosen response) and \(t_j^{(i)}\) (for the rejected response). These are concatenated with the original prompt and response to form enhanced inputs:

\[
x_{\text{chosen}} = p \oplus r_c \oplus t_c^{(1)} \oplus \dots \oplus t_c^{(N)},\quad
x_{\text{reject}} = p \oplus r_j \oplus t_j^{(1)} \oplus \dots \oplus t_j^{(N)}.
\]

A standard reward model (RM) then scores both enhanced inputs, producing \(s_c\) and \(s_j\). The Bradley‑Terry pairwise loss (Eqs. 7–8) trains the RM to predict human preferences.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Benchmarks**:  
  - **RM‑Bench** – instruction‑response pairs, evaluating factual accuracy.  
  - **JudgeBench** – response pairs with a focus on factual accuracy.  
  - **IFBENCH** – assesses instruction‑constrained responses (aligned with Peng et al., 2025).  
- **Baselines**:  
  - *Regression‑based RMs*: ArmoRM‑Llama3‑8B‑v0.1, INF‑ORM‑Llama3.1‑70B, Skywork‑Reward‑Llama‑3.1‑8B‑v0.2, internlm2‑reward.  
  - *Generative LLM‑based RMs*: GPT‑4o, GPT‑4o‑mini, Llama3‑8B‑Instruct.  
- **Evaluation Metrics**: Overall score computed as the average of RM‑Bench, JudgeBench, and the averaged score across the three subsets of IFBENCH.  
- **Training Details**: SBMs fine‑tuned with LoRA on LLaMA3‑8B; RM trained with Bradley‑Terry loss.  
- **Computational Efficiency**: Compared with GRM in Appendix D (not reported in the main text).

**Key observations**

- Adding side‑branch models boosts overall scores by **≈49.5 %** for Llama3‑8B‑Instruct (from 11.3 % to 60.8 %).  
- For internlm2‑7b‑reward, overall rises from 52.0 % to 63.1 % (+11.1 %).  
- For internlm2‑20b‑reward, overall rises from 56.4 % to 64.3 % (+7.9 %).  
- In RM‑Bench Normal, Llama3‑8B‑Instruct jumps from 9.3 % to 75.4 % (+66.1 %); Hard from 20.2 % to 39.5 % (+19.3 %).  
- Similar gains are seen for internlm2‑7b‑reward (Normal +5.8 %, Hard +26.9 %) and internlm2‑20b‑reward (Normal +4.7 %, Hard +21.3 %).  
- JudgeBench and IFBENCH also show consistent improvements across all models when side‑branch is applied.

No production or A/B‑test results are reported.
