# Summary

## TL;DR
The paper introduces a rigorous benchmarking framework for open‑source medical LLMs, evaluating **AlpaCare‑13B**, **BioMistral‑7B‑DARE**, and **Mistral‑7B** on a 1,077‑question dataset.  It reports trade‑offs between factual accuracy (*honesty*), usefulness (*helpfulness*), and safety (*harmlessness*), with AlpaCare‑13B achieving the highest accuracy (91.7 %) and harmlessness (0.92).  Domain‑specific tuning in BioMistral‑7B‑DARE boosts safety (0.90) while few‑shot prompting raises accuracy from 78 % to 85 %.  The study is industry‑relevant because it focuses on open‑source, regulated‑compliant models and provides a transparent, reproducible evaluation pipeline.

## Problem & Motivation
Large Language Models promise automated medical Q&A, but existing proprietary solutions (e.g., GPT‑4, Claude 3.5) are closed‑source, limiting transparency and regulatory compliance.  Open‑source models such as AlpaCare‑13B, BioMistral‑7B‑DARE, and Mistral‑7B lack systematic evaluation on real‑world medical questions.  The paper addresses the need for a benchmark that measures **honesty** (factual grounding), **helpfulness** (relevance and completeness), and **harmlessness** (absence of unsafe or misleading content) in a consumer‑health context.

## Approach
1. **Dataset construction** – Extract anatomy passages from standard textbooks and de‑identified clinical reports, clean with OCR (Tesseract v5.0+4, Google Cloud Vision OCR5), and cluster sentences by overlapping anatomical entities via a domain‑specific NER system.  
2. **QA generation** – Produce True/False pairs using:  
   * Rule‑based templates (e.g., “The {ANATOMICAL_ENTITY} is part of the {REGION_NAME}”).  
   * LLM prompting (GPT‑4) to synthesize natural statements.  
   * Edge‑case pattern filtering to remove unsafe or misleading content.  
3. **Annotation** – Three licensed physicians independently review a subset, achieving Cohen’s κ = 0.81.  Final set: 1,077 validated questions.  
4. **Evaluation protocol** – Three models are prompted with “Instruction: Given the following medical statement, determine whether it is TRUE or FALSE.”  Temperature = 0.7, top‑p = 0.9, max tokens = 128.  Responses are scored on *honesty*, *helpfulness*, and *harmlessness* using automated checks against the source corpus and safety‑critical question subsets.

## Experimental Setup
| Component | Details |
|-----------|----------|
| **Dataset** | 1,077 anatomy‑based True/False questions |
| **Models** | AlpaCare‑13B (13 B), BioMistral‑7B‑DARE (7 B), Mistral‑7B (7 B) |
| **Baselines** | Same three models; no proprietary baselines reported |
| **Metrics** | Honesty (factual consistency), Helpfulness (semantic alignment), Harmlessness (safety‑critical subset) |
| **Deployment context** | Offline evaluation with double‑blind A/B testing; no online deployment reported |
| **Hyperparameters** | Learning rate 1×10⁻⁵–5×10⁻⁵, batch 16–32, weight decay, early stopping after 3–5 epochs; inference temperature 0.7, top‑p 0.9, max 128 tokens |

## Results
| Model | Accuracy % | Honesty | Harmlessness |
|-------|------------|---------|--------------|
| Mistral‑7B | 82.5 | 0.78 | 0.81 |
| BioMistral‑7B‑DARE | 88.3 | 0.84 | 0.90 |
| AlpaCare‑13B | 91.7 | 0.89 | 0.92 |

*Table 1* (Accuracy & Honesty) and *Table 2* (Harmlessness) show that domain‑specific fine‑tuning yields higher factuality and safety.  
*Table 3* compares template‑generated vs. LLM‑generated questions: all models perform better on rule‑based templates (e.g., AlpaCare‑13B: Honesty 0.89 vs 0.86; Helpfulness 0.84 vs 0.78; Harmlessness 0.91 vs 0.87).  
*Table 4* stratifies helpfulness by complexity: Direct Fact Recall (DFR), Multi‑hop Inference (MHI), Negation/Compositional Logic (NEG/CL).  AlpaCare‑13B: DFR 0.91, MHI 0.84, NEG/CL 0.80; BioMistral‑7B‑DARE: DFR 0.87, MHI 0.77, NEG/CL 0.75; Mistral‑7B: DFR 0.80, MHI 0.68, NEG/CL 0.60.

The study also reports that few‑shot prompting improves accuracy from 78 % to 85 % (abstract), and that safety‑tuned BioMistral‑7B‑DARE achieves comparable harmlessness to the larger AlpaCare‑13B, indicating that scale alone is insufficient for safety.
