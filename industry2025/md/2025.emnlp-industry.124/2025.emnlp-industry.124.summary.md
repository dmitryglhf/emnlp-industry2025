# Summary

## TL;DR  
TACOS introduces a 21‑class taxonomy that unifies guardrails and tool‑calling for clinical chatbots. By training lightweight BERT classifiers on a TACOS‑annotated Korean dataset, the authors achieve higher accuracy and AUPRC than few‑shot prompting with GPT‑4o, Claude‑3.5‑Haiku, and Claude‑3.7‑Sonnet, while reducing inference latency from ~1 s to ~0.002 s per query. The work demonstrates that a domain‑specific taxonomy is essential for safe, efficient clinical agent deployment.

## Problem & Motivation  
Clinical LLM chatbots must avoid harmful or untruthful outputs, yet existing guardrails and tool‑calling systems are either too coarse (missing subtle clinical distinctions) or too fine‑grained (over‑categorizing toxic content). The authors argue that a single intent‑classification step can simultaneously decide safety handling and external‑tool selection, which is critical for high‑stakes medical interactions.

## Approach  
TACOS defines 21 intent classes that split queries into *safe* vs. *unsafe*, further distinguishing *clinical* vs. *non‑clinical*, and *information‑seeking* vs. *non‑information‑seeking*. The *information‑seeking* branch is subdivided into eight sub‑classes based on whether the query requires patient data, medical content, or app features. The taxonomy is language‑agnostic and designed to guide both guardrails and tool selection in one step. The authors curate a Korean dataset by:  
1. Collecting public user‑query corpora.  
2. Annotating with gpt‑4o‑mini according to TACOS.  
3. Generating additional queries for under‑represented classes with gpt‑4o‑mini.  
4. Having five human annotators review the full set.

## Experimental Setup  
- **Models**: GPT‑4o, Claude‑3.5‑Haiku, Claude‑3.7‑Sonnet (LLMs); m‑BERT, Ko‑BERT, Kr‑BERT, Klue‑BERT, Km‑BERT (BERT variants).  
- **Training**: Fine‑tune BERTs on 1,600 TACOS queries (200 per class) from the eight safe/clinical/information‑seeking categories.  
- **LLM Prompting**: Provide a list of the eight information‑seeking categories and definitions, with 0–100 shots.  
- **Metrics**: Accuracy, macro F1, macro AUPRC, inference time.  
- **Dataset Variants**: Balanced (500 examples per class, 10.5 K total), Imbalanced (random 10.5 K preserving skew), Imbalanced‑Large (21 K).  
- **Toxicity Study**: Use Korean UnSmile dataset (9 toxic sub‑categories). Train two BERT setups: *Total* (single toxic class) vs. *Separate* (nine toxic sub‑classes). Evaluate on a 21‑class test set.

- **Inference Time**: Fine‑tuned BERTs ≈0.002 s/query on NVIDIA TITAN RTX; GPT‑4o ≈1 s; Claude‑3.7‑Sonnet ≈13 s at 100 shots.  
- **LLM Accuracy**: GPT‑4o and Claude‑3.5‑Haiku reach higher AUPRC with ≥10 shots, but do not consistently surpass BERTs in accuracy/F1.  
- **Dataset Size Impact**: Training on *imbalanced* data yields comparable or slightly better accuracy/F1 than *balanced* data, and higher macro AUPRC. Doubling to *imbalanced‑large* (21 K) further improves all metrics.  
- **Toxicity Study**: Models trained on *total* (single toxic class) outperform *separate* (nine toxic sub‑classes) in accuracy, macro F1, and macro AUPRC, especially on non‑toxic classes, indicating that over‑categorization harms overall performance.

## Limitations  
- The taxonomy is evaluated only on Korean user queries; cross‑lingual generalization is untested.  
- The study relies on synthetic data generation (gpt‑4o‑mini) for under‑represented classes, which may introduce annotation bias.  
- The authors highlight that over‑specific labeling (e.g., nine toxic sub‑categories) degrades performance, suggesting a trade‑off between granularity and classifier effectiveness.  
- No real‑world deployment or A/B‑testing results are provided; practical safety guarantees remain to be validated in production settings.
